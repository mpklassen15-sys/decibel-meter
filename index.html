Measuring gunfire or suppressors? Read this first

This meter cannot rate suppressors — no phone or laptop app can. Consumer microphones overload around 110–120 dB, and gunshot impulses last under a millisecond. Suppressed gunfire still peaks around 130–145 dB, so a mic-based meter will under-read it. When your mic overloads, this app shows a red warning instead of pretending the number is real.

Where regular consumers can get real numbers:

  • PEW Science rankings — free independent lab data for specific suppressor + firearm + ammo combinations. The most trustworthy option, and it costs nothing.
  • Rent a firearm-rated meter for a test weekend — Larson Davis QPR kits from The Modal Shop or ATE Corp. Split the cost with your club.
  • Buy impulse-capable gear — a used B&K 2209 setup (≈$2,000, the hobbyist standard) for real readings, or a Cirrus Optimus Red (143 dB C-peak, ≈$500–800) for rough comparisons only.

Even well-suppressed gunfire sits near the 140 dB instantaneous hearing-damage threshold — wear hearing protection either way.

Typical levels

  • Whisper, quiet library30 dB
  • Normal conversation60 dB
  • City traffic, vacuum cleaner75 dB
  • Hearing damage risk (8 hr exposure)85 dB
  • Concert, power tools100 dB
  • Pain threshold120 dB
  • Suppressed gunshot (beyond mic range)130–145 dB
  • Unsuppressed rifle (beyond mic range)155–170 dB

Calibration

Offset +94 dB

While measuring, play a steady sound (a fan works), read the level off a reference meter — the free NIOSH SLM phone app is a good one — type it above and press Match. The offset is saved per microphone and restored automatically.
